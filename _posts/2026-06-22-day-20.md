  Starting off today, we began by completing our timesheets and making sure all of our information was correctly filled out and submitted. This helped ensure that our work hours and participation in the program were properly documented. After finishing our timesheets, we took a short break from research activities by playing a game as a group. Although my team did not win, I still enjoyed the activity because it allowed us to relax, interact with one another, and take a break before returning to our work. After our break, we continued working on Step 8 of our project. During this time, I worked alongside Asheley to better understand each section of the code so that I would be able to confidently explain it if asked. The code focused on loading a pretrained Vision Transformer (ViT) model and adapting it to classify buildings into four damage categories: No Damage, Minor Damage, Major Damage, and Destroyed. I learned that before training the model, satellite images must first be resized and augmented through techniques such as flipping, rotating, and adjusting brightness. These transformations help increase the diversity of the dataset and improve the model's ability to generalize to new disaster scenarios. I also learned how the images are organized into training and validation datasets using PyTorch DataLoaders, which efficiently provide batches of images to the model during training. Throughout the training process, the model continuously compares its predictions to the actual labels and adjusts its internal parameters to reduce errors and improve accuracy. Additionally, performance metrics such as accuracy and weighted F1-score are tracked using Weights & Biases (WandB), and the best-performing model is automatically saved for future evaluation. Overall, today's activities helped me gain a better understanding of how Vision Transformers are trained and evaluated for post-disaster building damage assessment.
