what_i_learned: |
  Today, we began by reviewing the tasks that our group needs to complete throughout the week. Our main goals include training all three models, completing our Overleaf writing assignments, preparing both our weekly and mid-symposium presentations, and beginning to develop ideas for our elevator pitch while practicing for the symposium. Reviewing these objectives helped me better understand the amount of work that still needs to be completed and the importance of staying organized as a team. For most of the day, I focused on completing portions of our literature review on satellite-based building damage assessment. Through my research, I learned that before the widespread use of deep learning, damage assessment primarily relied on traditional change detection methods and hand-crafted feature extraction techniques. These approaches often struggled with noise sensitivity and had difficulty generalizing across different disaster scenarios. I also learned that the introduction of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) significantly improved the field by allowing models to automatically extract important spatial features directly from satellite imagery. While reviewing recent studies, I learned that although deep learning models have achieved high levels of accuracy, many still struggle to generalize effectively to unseen geographic regions and disaster events. I examined research that focused on simplifying model architectures while maintaining strong performance, as well as studies that addressed challenges such as class imbalance, computational efficiency, and rapid inference during emergency response situations. Additionally, creating and completing the literature review chart allowed me to compare different studies based on their objectives, methodologies, strengths, limitations, and relevance to our own research. Overall, today's work gave me a deeper understanding of the current state of satellite-based building damage assessment research and highlighted important gaps that future studies still need to address.
